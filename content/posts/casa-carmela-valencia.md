If you live in Valencia long enough, you learn quickly which paella is real and which is a performance for tourists. The rule of thumb locals use: if the restaurant has a photo of paella on the front door and is within 200 meters of the cathedral, walk away.

**Casa Carmela** is the opposite of all that. It's been standing on the Malvarrosa seafront since **1922** — over a century — and it cooks paella the way it has always been cooked here: over burning orange wood, in wide flat pans, with rice from the Albufera lagoon just south of the city. No shortcuts, no gas burners, no gimmicks.

We finally went last Sunday. Andrei had been promised paella for approximately three weeks, so the pressure was on. 😄

![Traditional Valencian paella at Casa Carmela](/images/paella-valenciana.png)

## Getting there

Casa Carmela is at Carrer d'Isabel de Villena, 155 — right on the beach strip at Malvarrosa, about 4 km from our place. We cycled along the Turia park and down to the seafront, which is genuinely one of the nicest ways to spend a Sunday morning in this city. Locked up the bikes, walked in.

**Book ahead.** We reserved a table two weeks in advance and still barely got in. On a Sunday at 2pm the place was completely full — multigenerational Valencian families, couples, a few tourists who clearly did their homework. The room is bright, noisy, and alive. It smells incredible from the moment you walk in.

## What we ordered

We went for the full experience: **Paella Valenciana** (the real thing — chicken, rabbit, green beans, garrofó beans, no seafood — this is the original), plus a plate of **bacalà croquetes** to start, and **grilled octopus** because I am physically unable to pass up octopus.

The croquettes arrived golden and crackling, disappearing within about 90 seconds. The octopus was tender with a char on the outside, dressed with olive oil and smoked paprika. Simple. Perfect.

![Grilled octopus with lemon and paprika](/images/octopus-grilled.png)

Then the paella arrived.

I've eaten a lot of paella in Valencia. I've made paella at home with varying levels of success. I've eaten the tourist versions with frozen peas and questionable saffron. But this was something else. The rice had absorbed every bit of the broth — flavored with tomato, saffron, and whatever magic comes from wood smoke — and the bottom layer had formed a proper **socarrat**: that thin, slightly crispy crust that is the mark of a correctly cooked paella and the thing every Valencian grandmother judges you by.

We ate in silence for a moment. That's usually the sign.

## The verdict

**Honest review:** Casa Carmela is not cheap. Budget around 40–50€ per person for a proper meal. But it's not a splurge-once-and-feel-guilty place — it feels like exactly what it is: a family restaurant that has been doing one thing right for a hundred years, and charges accordingly.

The service is warm and unpretentious. Nobody tried to upsell us. They checked on the paella at the right moment (not too often, not too little). The wine list is short but well-chosen — we had a glass of local Bobal that worked beautifully with the rice.

Would I go back? Already planning the next visit. Thinking about the lobster rice. Andrei has been informed. ☀️
